# Diwali in Hamamatsu: How India's Festival of Lights Came to Shizuoka

When the nights grow longer in autumn, something magical happens in Hamamatsu. Oil lamps flicker to life, the scent of cardamom and ghee fills the air, and the sound of classical Indian music drifts through the city. Diwali—the festival of lights—has found a home in Shizuoka Prefecture, and Kumar Restaurant has been at the center of making it happen.

For members of Hamamatsu's Indian community, Diwali is the most important celebration of the year. But over the past decade, it's also become an event that Japanese neighbors, colleagues, and friends eagerly anticipate. The festival has evolved into a genuine cultural exchange—one plate of samosa, one rangoli pattern, one lamp at a time.

## What Is Diwali?

Diwali, sometimes spelled Deepavali, literally means "row of lights." It's a five-day festival celebrated by over a billion people across India, Nepal, Sri Lanka, and diaspora communities worldwide. At its core, Diwali celebrates the triumph of light over darkness, knowledge over ignorance, and good over evil.

The mythology behind Diwali varies by region. In northern India—where Mr. Binay Kumar's family comes from—Diwali commemorates the return of Lord Rama to his kingdom after 14 years of exile. The people of Ayodhya lit thousands of oil lamps to guide him home, and that tradition of illumination continues today.

Each of the five days has its own significance:

- **Day 1 — Dhanteras:** Cleaning and decorating homes, purchasing gold or silver
- **Day 2 — Naraka Chaturdashi:** Celebrating the defeat of the demon Narakasura
- **Day 3 — Diwali (main day):** The largest celebration, with prayers, feasts, and fireworks
- **Day 4 — Govardhan Puja:** Honoring the bond between humans and nature
- **Day 5 — Bhai Dooj:** Celebrating the relationship between brothers and sisters

For the Indian community in Hamamatsu, Diwali is a time to gather, celebrate, and share their culture with the broader community. It's a moment of joy that transcends cultural boundaries.

## Diwali in Japan

Japan's relationship with Indian culture has deepened significantly over the past two decades. The country's Indian population has grown steadily, and cities like Hamamatsu—with its large international workforce—have become multicultural hubs where diverse traditions coexist and intermingle.

Diwali celebrations in Japan began modestly, often organized by Indian community groups in major cities like Tokyo and Osaka. But as the Indian community expanded into smaller cities, Diwali followed. In Hamamatsu, the festival has grown from intimate home gatherings to public events that draw hundreds of attendees.

What makes Diwali in Japan unique is the cultural context. Japan already has a rich tradition of light festivals—from Nebuta in Aomori to the lantern festivals of Gifu and Nara. Japanese visitors often draw parallels between Diwali's oil lamps and their own festival lanterns, creating a natural point of cultural connection.

This resonance has helped Diwali gain acceptance and enthusiasm in Japan. It's not viewed as a foreign import but as an addition to the city's cultural calendar—a celebration that complements rather than competes with existing traditions.

Kumar Restaurant has played a significant role in this process. By hosting Diwali events and making the festival accessible to non-Indian attendees, we've helped bridge the cultural gap and create shared experiences that bring people together.

## Kumar Restaurant's Diwali Celebration

Each year, Kumar Restaurant hosts a Diwali celebration that has become one of the most anticipated events in Hamamatsu's cultural calendar. The event combines traditional Indian festivities with the welcoming spirit that defines our restaurant.

**Decorations and Atmosphere**

The transformation begins weeks before the event. Our team creates intricate rangoli patterns at the entrance—geometric designs made from colored powders that traditionally welcome guests and bring good luck. Inside, the restaurant is adorned with marigold garlands, silk draperies, and hundreds of small clay oil lamps (diyas) that create a warm, golden glow.

The effect is transportive. Guests often comment that stepping into the restaurant during Diwali feels like entering another world—a world of color, warmth, and celebration.

**Cultural Performances**

Our Diwali celebration features performances of several Indian classical dance forms:

- **Bharatanatyam** — A South Indian classical dance characterized by precise footwork and expressive gestures
- **Kathak** — A North Indian dance form known for its storytelling through movement and spinning
- **Odissi** — An ancient dance form from eastern India, known for its graceful, fluid movements

These performances are not just entertainment—they're windows into India's artistic heritage. Each dance tells a story, and our performers often explain the narratives before they begin, helping audiences appreciate the cultural significance of what they're watching.

**Community Participation**

One of the most heartwarming aspects of our Diwali celebration is how it brings together people from different backgrounds. Japanese guests learn to light diyas, Indian attendees share stories from their childhood celebrations, and children from both cultures create rangoli patterns together.

We've seen lasting friendships form at these events. The shared experience of celebration—of food, music, and light—creates bonds that transcend language and culture.
