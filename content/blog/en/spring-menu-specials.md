# Spring Menu Specials at Kumar Restaurant: Seasonal Flavors from India

Spring in Hamamatsu is a season of renewal. Cherry blossoms paint the city in pink, the air turns warm and fresh, and the markets fill with new-season produce. At Kumar Restaurant, we draw inspiration from this seasonal energy to create special dishes that celebrate the flavors of spring.

Our spring menu reflects a principle that's central to Indian culinary tradition: cooking with what's fresh, available, and at its peak. In India, seasons dictate the menu just as strongly as they do in Japan—spring brings specific vegetables, fruits, and ingredients that inspire dishes you won't find at other times of year.

Here's a look at what's special on our spring menu.

## Why Seasonal Cooking Matters

In an era of global supply chains and year-round availability, seasonal cooking might seem like an outdated concept. But at Kumar Restaurant, we believe it's more relevant than ever.

**Flavor**

Ingredients at their seasonal peak simply taste better. A tomato picked in summer has more flavor than one shipped across the world in winter. The same principle applies to the vegetables and herbs that form the foundation of Indian cooking.

**Nutrition**

Seasonal produce is typically harvested at its nutritional peak. The vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants in vegetables are highest when they're grown and consumed in their natural season.

**Sustainability**

Using seasonal ingredients reduces the environmental impact of transportation and storage. It also supports local farmers and suppliers who grow produce suited to Shizuoka's climate.

**Creativity**

Constraints breed creativity. Working with seasonal ingredients challenges our kitchen team to develop new dishes and find innovative ways to use what's available. Many of our most popular dishes originated as seasonal specials that became permanent menu additions.

## Spring Ingredients in Indian Cuisine

Spring holds a special place in Indian culinary tradition. The season marks the end of winter's rich, heavy foods and the beginning of lighter, brighter preparations. Here are some of the key spring ingredients that inspire our seasonal menu:

**Fresh Green Peas (Matar)**

Spring peas are sweeter and more tender than their frozen counterparts. In Indian cooking, fresh peas appear in curries, rice dishes, and stuffed breads. Their bright color and sweet flavor make them a natural spring ingredient.

**Spring Onions**

Young, mild spring onions replace the mature onions that dominate winter cooking. They add a fresh, slightly sharp flavor to dishes and are excellent in salads, chutneys, and light curries.

**New-Season Spinach**

Spring spinach is more tender and less bitter than winter varieties. It's the ideal base for palak paneer and other spinach-based dishes, contributing a sweeter, more delicate flavor.

**Raw Mango (Kairi)**

In India, raw (unripe) mango is a prized spring ingredient. Its tart, tangy flavor is used in chutneys, curries, and drinks. Raw mango adds a brightness that's perfect for spring dining.

**Fresh Coriander (Cilantro)**

Spring coriander is more aromatic and less overpowering than coriander grown in hotter months. It's essential in Indian cooking, appearing as a garnish, in chutneys, and blended into sauces.

## Our Spring Specials

This season, our kitchen team has developed several dishes that showcase spring's bounty:

**Spring Matar Paneer — ¥1,200**

Our classic matar paneer gets a spring upgrade. Fresh seasonal peas are combined with house-made paneer in a lightly spiced sauce that lets the peas' natural sweetness shine. Unlike our year-round version, this spring special uses minimal heavy cream—the fresh peas provide all the richness you need.

The dish is finished with a squeeze of lemon and a scattering of fresh coriander, adding brightness that echoes the season's energy.

**Raw Mango Curry — ¥1,100**

A seasonal special that's only available during spring. Raw mango pieces are cooked in a tangy, slightly sweet sauce with mustard seeds, curry leaves, and green chilies. The result is a dish that's simultaneously sour, sweet, and spicy—a flavor profile that captures the complexity of Indian cooking.

This curry pairs beautifully with steamed basmati rice and a side of cooling yogurt.

**Spring Vegetable Biryani — ¥1,400**

Our spring biryani features seasonal vegetables at their peak: fresh peas, baby carrots, spring onions, and tender spinach leaves. Layered with fragrant basmati rice, saffron, and our house spice blend, it's a celebration of spring's colors and flavors.

The dum pukht (slow-cooking) method allows the vegetables to release their juices into the rice, creating a dish where every grain is infused with seasonal flavor.

**Green Papaya Salad with Indian Spices — ¥800**

A refreshing starter that bridges Indian and Southeast Asian flavors. Shredded green papaya is tossed with mustard seeds, dried red chilies, lime juice, and a touch of jaggery (unrefined sugar). It's light, crunchy, and the perfect way to begin a spring meal.

**Mango Lassi — ¥450**

While mango lassi is available year-round, our spring version uses the first Alphonso mangoes of the season—considered the king of mangoes in India. The flavor is more intense and aromatic than the standard version, making it a spring treat worth savoring.

**Spring Thali — ¥2,500**

## How Spring Differs from Other Seasons

To appreciate our spring menu fully, it helps to understand how Indian seasonal cooking evolves throughout the year:

**Winter (December-February)**

Winter in Indian cooking is rich and warming. Heavy cream, butter, and ghee feature prominently. Dishes are slow-cooked and deeply spiced. Think: butter chicken, dal makhani, and rich kormas.

**Spring (March-May)**

Spring lightens the menu. Fresh vegetables, lighter sauces, and brighter flavors take center stage. Rich dishes give way to preparations that celebrate the season's produce.

**Summer (June-August)**

Summer brings cooling foods: yogurt-based dishes, fresh salads, and light curries. Raw mango chutneys and spiced buttermilk are summer staples.

**Autumn (September-November)**

Autumn marks the transition back to heartier fare. Fresh harvest vegetables appear, and dishes begin to grow richer in preparation for winter.

At Kumar, our year-round menu reflects these seasonal shifts. The spring specials you'll find now won't be available in summer—they're a celebration of this particular moment in the culinary calendar.
